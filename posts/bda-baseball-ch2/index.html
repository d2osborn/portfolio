BDA & Baseball, Chapter 1: Probability and Inference

2026-06-17

This is the second entry in a series where I work through Andrew Gelman's Bayesian Data Analysis (3rd edition) one chapter at a time, summarizing the key ideas and then applying them to baseball data.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 2 of BDA3 introduces the three core steps of Bayesian data analysis:

  1. Setting up a full probability model — a joint distribution over all observable and unobservable quantities consistent with our knowledge about the problem.
  2. Conditioning on observed data — computing the posterior distribution of the unobserved quantities we care about.
  3. Evaluating the model fit — checking the implications of the posterior, and deciding whether the model is adequate.

The fundamental equation underpinning everything is Bayes' theorem:

$$p(\theta \mid y) = \frac{p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)}{p(y)}$$

where $\theta$ represents our parameters, $y$ is the observed data, $p(\theta)$ is the prior, $p(y \mid \theta)$ is the likelihood, and $p(\theta \mid y)$ is the posterior.

Gelman emphasizes that the posterior distribution is the complete answer to any Bayesian inference question — it encodes everything we know about $\theta$ after observing the data. Point estimates, intervals, and predictions all flow from the posterior.

Baseball Application: Estimating a Batting Average

Let's apply this framework to one of the most natural problems in baseball analytics: estimating a player's "true" batting average after observing some at-bats early in the season.

The setup

Suppose a hitter is 8-for-25 through his first week of the season, batting .320. Should we believe he's a .320 hitter? Probably not — small sample sizes are noisy. This is where Bayesian thinking shines.

We model each at-bat as a Bernoulli trial with success probability $\theta$ (the "true" batting average). Given $n$ at-bats with $y$ hits:

$$y \mid \theta \sim \text{Binomial}(n, \theta)$$

Choosing a prior

From historical MLB data, we know that batting averages tend to cluster around .250 with relatively little spread. A reasonable prior is:

$$\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$

If we set $\alpha = 50$ and $\beta = 150$, the prior mean is $\frac{50}{200} = .250$ — right at the league average — with a standard deviation of about .031, which comfortably covers the range of most hitters (.200 to .320 or so).

The posterior

Because the Beta distribution is conjugate to the Binomial, the posterior is also Beta:

$$\theta \mid y \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + y, \, \beta + n - y) = \text{Beta}(58, 167)$$

The posterior mean is $\frac{58}{225} \approx .258$ — the model "shrinks" the observed .320 back toward the league average of .250, which is exactly the kind of regularization we want this early in the season.

Code

Here's a quick visualization in Python: 

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import beta

theta = np.linspace(0, 0.5, 500)

# Prior: Beta(50, 150)
prior = beta.pdf(theta, 50, 150)

# Likelihood: Binomial(25, 8) — proportional
likelihood = beta.pdf(theta, 8 + 1, 25 - 8 + 1)

# Posterior: Beta(58, 167)
posterior = beta.pdf(theta, 58, 167)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 4.5))
ax.plot(theta, prior, label="Prior: Beta(50, 150)", ls="--", alpha=0.7)
ax.plot(theta, likelihood, label="Likelihood (scaled)", ls=":", alpha=0.7)
ax.plot(theta, posterior, label="Posterior: Beta(58, 167)", lw=2)
ax.axvline(8 / 25, color="gray", ls="--", alpha=0.4, label="Observed AVG (.320)")
ax.set_xlabel(r"$\theta$ (batting average)")
ax.set_ylabel("Density")
ax.set_title("Bayesian Estimation of Batting Average")
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("ch1_batting_avg.png", dpi=150)
plt.show()

What we learn

Even with this simple conjugate model, we see the core Bayesian machinery at work:

  • The prior encodes what we already know about batting averages league-wide.
  • The likelihood tells us what this specific player's data says.
  • The posterior blends both sources of information, weighted by their relative precision.

As the season progresses and $n$ grows, the likelihood dominates and the posterior concentrates around the player's actual ability. But early on, the prior does important work — it prevents us from overreacting to a hot (or cold) start.

Key Takeaway

Bayesian inference is just the formal, probabilistic version of something baseball people do intuitively: you don't believe a guy is a .400 hitter after 20 at-bats. Bayes' theorem makes that intuition rigorous.
